## Red Cell Distribution Width (RDW)


Red Blood Cell Distribution Width (RDW) is a measurement of the variation in volume and size of red blood cells (erythrocytes). Red blood cells are made in the bone marrow, the spongy tissue inside large bones, and contain hemoglobin, an iron-rich protein that carries oxygen from the lungs to every cell in the body. Cells need oxygen to grow, reproduce, and produce energy for proper function. Normally, red blood cells are roughly the same size, and significant size differences can affect how efficiently oxygen is delivered throughout the body. An RDW test uses a special graph called a histogram to visualize this variation. When red blood cells are similar in size, they appear close together on the histogram, resulting in a low RDW. A high RDW indicates greater size variation than normal, with values more spread out on the histogram, which may be a sign of an underlying medical condition.
